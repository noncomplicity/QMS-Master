## Clinical Evaluation
The Medical Device Regulation (MDR) 2017/745 requires that medical devices undergo clinical evaluation before they can be placed on the market. The clinical evaluation is a systematic and ongoing process that assesses the clinical safety and performance of the device.

To meet the MDR requirements, manufacturers must prepare a Clinical Evaluation Report (CER) that provides a comprehensive analysis of clinical data and relevant literature on the device. The CER must include a detailed description of the device and its intended use, a critical evaluation of the clinical data, an analysis of clinical risks and benefits, and a summary of the overall clinical evidence.

The clinical data used in the evaluation must come from clinical investigations or other sources, such as post-market surveillance, clinical experience, or scientific literature. The data must be relevant to the device and its intended use, and should be of sufficient quality and quantity to demonstrate the safety and performance of the device.

The MDR also requires that the clinical evaluation be updated throughout the life cycle of the device, based on the results of post-market surveillance, changes to the device or its intended use, and new clinical data or scientific literature. The manufacturer must ensure that the device remains safe and effective over its lifetime and take appropriate measures if any safety or performance issues arise.

In summary, the MDR 2017/745 requires that medical device manufacturers conduct a thorough and ongoing clinical evaluation of their devices to ensure their safety and performance throughout their life cycle.

## Valid clinical association
There may be situations where clinical data as defined in MDR 2017/745 is not available or feasible to obtain for a medical device software. In such cases, the use of relevant scientific literature applying MDCG 2020-1 can provide an alternative approach to demonstrate the safety and performance of the device.

The MDCG 2020-1 guidance provides a framework for manufacturers to use scientific literature to support the clinical evaluation of their devices. This approach involves identifying and analyzing relevant scientific literature that demonstrates the safety and performance of the device, and then using a valid clinical association to link the device to the literature.

This approach may be appropriate for medical device software where clinical trials may not be feasible or may not provide sufficient data to demonstrate the safety and performance of the device. For example, in the case of a medical device software used for patient monitoring or data analysis, relevant scientific literature on the clinical use of similar devices or technologies could be used to support the safety and performance of the software.

It is important to note that the use of scientific literature must be done in accordance with MDCG 2020-1 and the literature must be of high quality, relevant to the device, and provide a sufficient level of evidence to support the claims made about the device. Additionally, a valid clinical association must be established between the device and the literature to demonstrate its relevance to the device's intended use and clinical performance.

In summary, the use of relevant scientific literature applying MDCG 2020-1 can provide an alternative approach to demonstrate the safety and performance of medical device software when clinical data as defined in MDR 2017/745 is not available or feasible to obtain. However, this approach must be done in accordance with the guidelines and the literature must be of high quality and provide a sufficient level of evidence to support the claims made about the device.

## Device Technical Performance
Valid clinical associations between medical device software and relevant scientific literature provide a foundation for demonstrating the safety and performance of the device. However, to further support these associations, validation of technical performance and usability of the device is also important.

Validation of technical performance involves testing the device's functionality, accuracy, and reliability in a controlled environment to ensure that it performs as intended. This can include testing for factors such as data accuracy, signal stability, and software functionality. Validating technical performance can help to ensure that the device is safe and effective for its intended use, and can provide additional evidence to support the valid clinical association between the device and relevant scientific literature.

Usability testing involves assessing the device's ease of use and the user experience, and can provide valuable information on how the device is used in a real-world setting. This can include testing for factors such as user interface design, user instructions, and user feedback. Validating usability can help to ensure that the device is user-friendly and that users can use it safely and effectively, which can also support the valid clinical association between the device and relevant scientific literature.

In summary, while a valid clinical association between medical device software and relevant scientific literature is essential to demonstrate the safety and performance of the device, validation of technical performance and usability can provide additional evidence to support this association. Validating technical performance and usability can help to ensure that the device is safe, effective, and user-friendly for its intended use, which can further strengthen the evidence base for the device's safety and performance.

## Device Clinical Performance
The conversion of technical performance to clinical performance is an important step in demonstrating the safety and effectiveness of medical device software. Technical performance validation provides evidence that the device can perform as intended in a controlled environment, but it is important to also demonstrate that this performance translates to real-world clinical settings.

This is where the valid clinical association between the device and relevant scientific literature plays a crucial role. By establishing a valid clinical association between the device and the literature, it is possible to draw a connection between the device's technical performance and its clinical performance.

For example, if a medical device software is designed to analyze patient data and provide diagnostic recommendations, technical performance validation can demonstrate that the device can accurately process and analyze data. However, to demonstrate that the device can improve patient outcomes, it is necessary to link the device's technical performance to clinical outcomes supported by relevant scientific literature. This can include demonstrating that the device can accurately identify patients with certain conditions or that it can lead to more accurate diagnoses and treatment recommendations.

By converting technical performance to clinical performance through the valid clinical association, it is possible to demonstrate that the device can perform as intended in real-world clinical settings and can lead to improved patient outcomes. This can help to further strengthen the evidence base for the device's safety and effectiveness and provide confidence in its use for its intended purpose.

In summary, the conversion of technical performance to clinical performance is essential to demonstrate the safety and effectiveness of medical device software. The valid clinical association between the device and relevant scientific literature plays a crucial role in linking the device's technical performance to clinical outcomes, providing a clear and evidence-based path to demonstrate the device's clinical performance in real-world settings.

## Benefits and Risks in this approach
The approach of using scientific literature and establishing a valid clinical association, followed by validation of technical performance and usability, and conversion of technical performance to clinical performance, can be a feasible way to comply with the MDR requirements on clinical evaluation for medical device software. However, it is important to note that this approach must be done in accordance with the MDR requirements and applicable guidance documents, and the evidence presented must be robust, objective, and reliable.

One potential risk in this approach is the quality of the scientific literature used to establish the valid clinical association. The literature must be of high quality, relevant to the device, and provide a sufficient level of evidence to support the claims made about the device. There is a risk that insufficient or poor quality literature could lead to incorrect conclusions about the device's clinical performance, which could impact patient safety.

Another potential risk is the validity of the technical performance and usability testing. It is important to ensure that these tests are conducted in a rigorous and objective manner, using appropriate methodologies and validated test protocols. Failure to conduct thorough and objective technical and usability testing could result in incorrect conclusions about the device's safety and effectiveness.

Overall, while this approach can be a feasible way to comply with MDR requirements on clinical evaluation, it is important to carefully consider the evidence presented and ensure that it meets the required standards for reliability, objectivity, and robustness. Manufacturers should work closely with competent authorities, notified bodies, and other stakeholders to ensure that their approach is appropriate and in line with regulatory requirements.
