1.  Purpose

The purpose with this document (SOP) is to describe the process for clinical evaluation of Kontigo Care medical devices which involves reviewing data generated through literature search, clinical experience and from clinical investigations, as well as the actual writing of the clinical evaluation report. In addition, the process of Post Market Clinical Follow-up (PMCF) is described and writing the Summary of Safety and Clinical Performance (SSCP), as appropriate. PMCF and SSCP (if applicable) may be issued separate.

Clinical evaluation means a systematic and planned process to continuously generate, collect, analyze and evaluate the clinical data pertaining to a device to verify the safety and performance, including clinical benefits, of the device when used as intended by the manufacturer. Demonstration of conformity with the general safety and performance requirements (GSPR) of the medical device regulation (EU) 2017/745 shall include a clinical evaluation. Clinical evaluation is conducted throughout the lifecycle of the medical devices, as a continuously ongoing process.

The clinical evaluation shall be performed in accordance with the guidance provided in:

-   MEDDEV 2.7/1 Revision 4, Clinical Evaluation A Guide for Manufacturers and Notified Bodies under Directives 93/42/EEC and 90/385/EEC[^1]

[^1]: This guidance is based on the Medical Devices Directive 93/42/EEC, but until it is replaced by a MDCG guidance document, all applicable parts are valid also for MDR. MDR or other MDCG guidance documents overrule MEDDEV 2.7/1 where there are differences.

And if applicable for the product, the following guidelines should be used:

-   MDCG 2020-1 Guidance on Clinical Evaluation (MDR) / Performance Evaluation (IVDR) of Medical Device Software
-   MDCG 2020-5 Clinical Evaluation – Equivalence A guide for manufacturers and notified bodies
-   MDCG 2020-6 Regulation (EU) 2017/745: Clinical evidence needed for medical devices previously CE marked under Directives 93/42/EEC or 90/385/EEC

## Limitation/exclusion

Clinical investigations are outside the scope of this SOP.

1.  Scope

The current scope for Kontigo Care is class IIa and class I medical devices.

Implants, custom made devices and devices without a medical purpose are out of scope. Parts which are out of scope and thus not applied are included but marked as “N/A” and the text written in light grey text.

1.  Responsibility

The R&D manager is responsible for implementing and maintaining this SOP and for appointment of appropriate evaluators. The R&D manager may assign experts for performing the clinical evaluation. The clinical evaluation, PMCF or SSCP should be conducted by a suitably qualified individual or a team, taking the following aspects into consideration:

-   Requirements shall be defined for the evaluators that are in line with the nature of the device under evaluation and its clinical performance and risks.
-   Justification shall be provided for the choice of the evaluators through reference to their qualifications and documented experience, and to present a declaration of interest for each evaluator.
-   As a general principle, the evaluators should possess knowledge of the following:
    -   research methodology (including clinical investigation design and biostatistics);
    -   information management (e.g. scientific background or librarianship qualification; experience with relevant databases);
    -   regulatory requirements; and
    -   medical writing (e.g. post-graduate experience in a relevant science or in medicine; training and experience in medical writing, systematic and clinical data appraisal).
-   With respect to the under evaluation, the evaluators should in addition have knowledge of:
    -   the device technology and its application;
    -   diagnosis and management of the conditions intended to be diagnosed or managed by
    -   the device, knowledge of medical alternatives, treatment standards and technology (e.g. specialist clinical expertise in the relevant medical specialty).
-   The evaluators should have at least the following training and experience in the relevant field:
    -   a degree from higher education in the respective field and 5 years of documented professional experience; or
    -   10 years of documented professional experience if a degree is not a prerequisite for a given task.
    -   The evaluator with specialist clinical expertise in the relevant medical specialty should be a trained and experienced physician. Usually this is a trained physician.

There may be circumstances where the level of evaluator expertise may be less or different; this should be documented and duly justified.

1.  Process

![En bild som visar text, skärmbild, diagram, linje Automatiskt genererad beskrivning](media/c2836d613a6e4cfb44ee8a0fbd545c43.jpg)

The clinical evaluation is based on a comprehensive analysis of available pre- and post-market clinical data relevant to the intended purpose of the device in question, including clinical performance data and clinical safety data.

There are discrete stages in performing a clinical evaluation:

-   **Stage 0:** Define the scope, plan the clinical evaluation (also referred to as scoping and the clinical evaluation plan).
-   **Stage 1:** Identify pertinent data.
-   **Stage 2:** Appraise each individual data set, in terms of its scientific validity, relevance and weighting.
-   **Stage 3:** Analyze the data, whereby conclusions are reached about- compliance with the General Safety and Performance Requirements (GSPR) on performance and safety of the device, including its benefit/risk profile,  
     - the contents of information materials supplied by the manufacturer (including the label, IFU of the device, available promotional materials, including accompanying documents possibly foreseen by the manufacturer),  
     - residual risks and uncertainties or unanswered questions (including on rare complications, long term performance, safety under wide-spread use), whether these are acceptable for CE-marking, and whether they are required to be addressed during PMS and/or PMCF.
-   **Stage 4:** Finalize the clinical evaluation report.

    The clinical evaluation report summarises and draws together the evaluation of all the relevant clinical data documented or referenced in other parts of the technical documentation. The clinical evaluation report and the relevant clinical data constitute the clinical evidence for conformity assessment.

The stages are often iterative where the appraisal and analysis stage may uncover and raise new questions, with a need to widen the scope of the evaluation, refine the clinical evaluation plan, and to retrieve, appraise and analyse additional data.

3.  Background on Clinical Evaluation

The risk management system should be aligned with and reflected in the clinical evaluation for the device, including the clinical risks to be addressed as part of clinical investigations, clinical evaluation and post-market clinical follow up.

The clinical evaluation shall be thorough, objective and consider both favourable and unfavourable data. Its depth and extent shall be in proportion and appropriate to the nature, classification, intended purpose and risks of the device in question, as well as to the claims in respect of the device.

According to Article 61 of the medical device regulation (EU) 2017/745 – “the MDR” (Ref 1) clinical evaluation shall follow a defined and methodologically sound procedure based on the following:

1.  a critical evaluation of the relevant scientific literature currently available relating to the safety, performance, design characteristics and intended purpose of the device, where the following conditions are satisfied:
-   it is demonstrated that the device subject to clinical evaluation for the intended purpose is equivalent to the device to which the data relate in accordance with paragraph 3 of Annex XIV (see Ref 4 for guidance on equivalence)
-   the data adequately demonstrate compliance with the relevant general safety and performance requirements
1.  a critical evaluation of the results of all available clinical data including clinical investigations, taking duly into consideration whether the investigations were performed under Articles 62 to 80, any acts adopted pursuant to article 81, Annex XV, which may be in the form of a GAP analysis towards ISO 14155, as applicable.
2.  a consideration of currently available alternative treatment options for that purpose, if any.

The clinical evidence together with non-clinical data generated from non-clinical testing methods and other relevant documentation shall allow for demonstration of conformity with the general safety and performance requirements and shall be part of the technical documentation for the device, except for custom-made devices.

The full list of considerations described in Article 61 of the MDR should always be reviewed to see which considerations are applicable to the device under investigation, and what is required for each one.

The following sections describes specific cases to be considered depending on product type. Only the cases applicable for the product under evaluation should be considered during the actual clinical evaluation.

1.  **N/A** Clinical data for implantable and class III devices

In the case of implantable devices and class III devices, clinical investigations shall be performed, except if:

1.  the device has been designed by modifications of a device already marketed by the same manufacturer,
2.  the modified device has been demonstrated by the manufacturer to be equivalent to the marketed device[^2] (criteria of equivalence according to paragraph 3, Annex XIV) and this demonstration has been endorsed by the notified body, and
3.  the clinical evaluation of the marketed device is sufficient to demonstrate conformity of the modified device with the relevant safety and performance requirements. In this case, the plan for Post Market Clinical Follow up (PMCF) shall include post market studies to demonstrate the safety and performance of the device.

[^2]: A marketed device is considered to be a device already placed on the market and CE marked with respect to either the MDR or the directives 93/42/EEC or 90/385/EEC. The CE marking should still be valid, should be based on an updated clinical evaluation, and the benefit/risk ratio for this device should be favourable (Ref 5).

In addition, clinical investigations need not be performed for class III devices in the cases referred to in paragraph 6 (Article 61), where:

1.  which have been lawfully placed on the market or put into service in accordance with Directive 90/385/EEC or Directive 93/42/EEC and for which the clinical evaluation:
-   is based on sufficient clinical data, and
-   is in compliance with the relevant product-specific common specification for the clinical evaluation of that kind of device where such a common specification is available;
1.  that are sutures, staples, dental fillings, dental braces, tooth crowns, screws, wedges, plates, wires, pins, clips or connectors for which the clinical evaluation is based on sufficient clinical data and is in compliance with the relevant product-specific common specification, where such a common specification is available.

The devices listed in b) are considered as “well established technologies” but the term is not restricted to only the devices listed. The common features of the devices which are well established technologies are that they all have:

-   relatively simple, common and stable designs with little evolution;
-   their generic device group has well-known safety and has not been associated with safety issues in the past;
-   well-known clinical performance characteristics and their generic device group are standard of care devices where there is little evolution in indications and the state of the art;
-   a long history on the market.

Therefore, any devices that meet all these criteria may be considered “well established technologies” (Ref 5).

1.  Legacy devices

A guidance for clinical data needed for devices previously CE marked under Directives 93/42/EEC or 90/385/EEC is given in Ref 5, also known as Legacy devices. For the first MDR conformity assessment of a legacy device the pre-market and post-market clinical data generated for the purpose of MDD/AIMDD can be considered. As requirements and guidance developed over time, it is not necessarily the case that the clinical data used for conformity assessment under the Directives is clinical data providing sufficient clinical evidence for the purpose of MDR requirements. Therefore, PMCF data together with the clinical data generated for the conformity assessment under the MDD/AIMDD will be the basis of the clinical evaluation process for legacy devices under the MDR.

1.  **CURRENTLY N/A** Products Without an Intended an Intended Medical Purpose

In the case of the products without an intended medical purpose listed in Annex XVI of MDR, the requirement to demonstrate a clinical benefit shall be understood as a requirement to demonstrate the performance of the device. Demonstration of clinical benefits is made in accordance with Chapter VI, Article 61, and Annexes XIV and XV. Clinical evaluations of products without an intended purpose shall be based on relevant data concerning safety, including data from post-market surveillance, PMCF, and, where applicable, specific clinical investigations. Clinical investigations shall be performed for these products unless reliance on existing clinical data from an analogous medical device is duly justified. Common specifications shall be followed if available.

1.  Use of Equivalent Devices

It is possible to rely on data for an already marketed equivalent device to not perform a clinical investigation, provided that the following conditions are fulfilled:

-   The legal manufacturer of the device under evaluation has full access to the technical documentation on an ongoing basis from the other company. For implantable devices and class III devices a contract needs to be in place between the manufacturers that explicitly allows access to the required data, and
-   the original clinical evaluation has been performed in compliance with the requirements in the MDR, and the other company provides clear evidence thereof to the notified body.

The following technical, biological and clinical characteristics shall be taken into consideration for the demonstration of equivalence according to Annex XIV, Part A and paragraph 3:

-   Technical:

    The device is of similar design; is used under similar conditions of use; has similar specifications and properties including physicochemical properties such as intensity of energy, tensile strength, viscosity, surface characteristics, wavelength and software algorithms; uses similar deployment methods, where relevant; has similar principles of operation and critical performance requirements;

-   Biological:

    The device uses the same materials or substances in contact with the same human tissues or body fluids for a similar kind and duration of contact and similar release characteristics of substances, including degradation products and leachables. In certain cases, GSPR 12.2 must be considered for biological equivalence;

-   Clinical:

    The device is used for the same clinical condition or purpose, including similar severity and stage of disease, at the same site in the body, in a similar population, including as regards age, anatomy and physiology; has the same kind of user; has similar relevant critical performance in view of the expected clinical effect for a specific intended purpose.

The characteristics shall be similar to the extent that there would be no clinically significant difference in the safety and clinical performance of the device. Considerations of equivalence shall be based on proper scientific justification. It shall be clearly demonstrated that there are sufficient levels of access to the data relating to devices with which equivalence is claimed in order to justify claims of equivalence. For further guidance, see Ref 4.

1.  Scoping and Clinical Evaluation Plan

Before a clinical evaluation is undertaken the manufacturer should define its scope, based on the GSPRs that need to be addressed from a clinical perspective and the nature and history of the device. This is also referred to as scoping, see MEDDEV 2.7/1, rev.4 (Ref 2).

The scope serves as a basis for further steps, including the identification of pertinent data. A description of the device under evaluation should be set up based on relevant technical documentation and included in the Clinical Evaluation Plan (CEP).

The clinical evaluation plan shall include, at least (Annex XIV – part A, MDR):

-   an identification of the general safety and performance requirements that require support from relevant clinical data;[^3]
-   a device description, including any design features, indications or target populations that require specific attention
-   a specification of the intended purpose of the device;
-   a clear specification of intended target groups with clear indications and contra-indications;
-   a detailed description of intended clinical benefits to patients with relevant and specified clinical outcome parameters;
-   a specification of methods to be used for examination of qualitative and quantitative aspects of clinical safety with clear reference to the determination of residual risks and side-effects;
-   an indicative list and specification of parameters to be used to determine, based on the state of the art in medicine, the acceptability of the benefit-risk ratio for the various indications and for the intended purpose or purposes of the device;
-   an indication how benefit-risk issues relating to specific components such as use of pharmaceutical, nonviable animal or human tissues, are to be addressed; and
-   a clinical development plan indicating progression from exploratory investigations, such as first-in-man studies, feasibility and pilot studies, to confirmatory investigations, such as pivotal clinical investigations, and a PMCF (Part B of Annex XIV) with an indication of milestones and a description of potential acceptance criteria[^4][^5].

[^3]: In general GSPR 1 and 8 are always applicable (article 61 paragraph 1). Other GSPRs can be applicable depending on product type.

[^4]: Premarket elements of the plan as described in the final indent of MDR Annex XIV Section 1a (first-in-man studies, feasibility and pilot studies) are not generally relevant to legacy devices which are unchanged in design or indications. However, the basis for the PMCF as described in indent 8 of MDR Annex XIV Section 1a are considered relevant and necessary for demonstration of compliance to the MDR.

[^5]: Since state of the art, applicable clinical outcome parameters and clinical risks might not be fully known in the planning stage, the clinical development plan might have to be updated after the first clinical evaluation has been performed.

There shall also be consideration of:

-   Whether data from equivalent devices can be used to support the safety and/or performance of the device in question and the information required.
-   The risk management documents of the device.
-   The data source(s) and type(s) of data to be used in the clinical evaluation. See the section “Data Sources” below for a description of different data sources.
-   Whether changes have been or are intended to be introduced, including design changes, changes to materials and manufacturing procedures, changes to the information materials supplied and whether the claim of equivalence to an existing device is (still) appropriate.
-   Whether there are any specific clinical concerns that have newly emerged and need to be addressed.
-   PMS and PMCF aspects that need to be updated regularly in the clinical evaluation report, such as new clinical data for the device under evaluation, new clinical data for equivalent devices (if equivalence is claimed), new knowledge about potential hazards, performance etc. e.g. data on clinical hazards in other products, changes concerning current knowledge/state of the art or other aspects identified during PMS and/or PMCF.
-   If the evaluation concerns a legacy device (a device previously marketed under MDD)
1.  Identification of data

The Evaluator shall identify data relevant to the device and determine the types and amount of data needed for the clinical evaluation according to the CEP. The data identified as relevant for safety and/or performance shall be documented.

Regarding the scientific literature searches as a means of collecting data, the pre-defined search terms, screening criteria (inclusion and exclusion), evaluations and the appraisal of literature data is specified in a separate document, a Literature Search Protocol, LSP. The results from the literature searches and selections are then documented in a separate Literature Search Report, LSR.

After the gathering of all identified data, each data set shall be evaluated according to the criteria defined in the LSP. The outcome of the evaluation shall be documented.

1.  Data Generated and Held by the Manufacturer

Data may include the following items (list is not exhaustive):

-   All pre-market clinical investigations
-   All clinical data generated from risk management activities and the PMS programs which have been implemented in Europe and other countries, e.g. PMCF studies, PMS reports, PSUR´s, incident reports, complaints, field safety corrective actions and other use reports
-   Relevant pre-clinical studies, e.g. biological and technical testing, bench testing, including verification and validation

All data need to be identified and complete data needs to be disclosed and made available to the evaluators.

1.  Data Reviewed from Literature

Literature searching identifies potential sources of clinical data for establishing:

-   Clinical data relevant to the device under evaluation (data may relate either to the device under evaluation or to an equivalent device or a benchmark device)
-   Current knowledge/state of the art (includes standards, guidance documents, scientific reviews, common specifications, data that relate to benchmark devices, other devices, critical components and available treatment alternatives to that of the device)
-   Incident data retrieved from incident databases, Competent Authorities, etc, that relate to the objects mentioned above[^6].

[^6]: Usually performed as part of the PMS.

Methods for retrieving data should always be pre-defined, e.g. regarding databases to be employed, search terms, inclusion- and exclusion criteria, and methods for evaluating and appraising selected data. The searches should be documented to such degree that they can be reproduced.

1.  Data Appraisal

Appraise each included individual data set, in terms of its scientific validity, relevance and weighting. The following should be performed:

-   Identification of information contained in each document
-   Evaluation of the methodological quality of work and the scientific validity
-   Determination of relevance of the information
-   Systematic weighting of the contribution of each data set
1.  Analysis of Data

Analyze the data, whereby conclusions are reached about:

-   compliance with GSPR including the benefit/risk profile of the device under investigation,
-   the contents of information materials supplied by the manufacturer (including the label, IFU of the device, available promotional materials, including accompanying documents possibly foreseen by the manufacturer),
-   residual risks and uncertainties or unanswered questions (including on rare complications, long term performance, safety under wide-spread use), whether these are acceptable for CE-marking, and whether they are required to be addressed during PMCF.

In conjunction with the PMS process the need for PMCF shall be evaluated. PMCF shall be considered for products where identification of possible emerging risks and the evaluation of long-term safety and performance are critical and cannot be detected solely in pre-market clinical investigations, where the time for follow-up is relatively short. Where the clinical evaluation has been done solely using equivalent devices as source of clinical data, PMCF is usually needed if not a valid justification can be given. The manufacturer is responsible and should therefore be part of the decision of what kind of and if a PMCF is warranted.

The outcome of the evaluation of clinical data shall be reviewed. The data sets that are identified as pivotal in the evaluation shall be explored looking for consistency of results across particular device performance characteristics and identified risks. If the different data sets report similar outcomes, certainty about the performance increases. If different results are observed across the data sets, it will be helpful to determine the reasons for such differences. The appraisal of different articles may also aid in this process, where data sets with better grading should be regarded as having more weight in the assessment of data.

The evaluator shall consider the basis on which it can be demonstrated that the combined data show that:

-   the device performs as intended by the manufacturer,
-   the device does not pose any undue safety concerns to either the recipient or end-user,
-   any risks associated with the use of the device are acceptable when weighed against the benefits to the patient,
-   the device has been assessed in relation to current knowledge/state of the art.

If it cannot be concluded that the acquired documentation is sufficient to support the above, new or additional data shall be generated. The new data shall be assessed according to the same criteria as the data identified earlier.

If the clinical evidence is considered sufficient to declare conformity with the relevant General Safety and Performance Requirements, a clinical evaluation report shall be written.

1.  The Clinical Evaluation Report

A Clinical Evaluation Report (CER) shall be written that outlines the scope and context of the evaluation; the inputs (clinical data); the appraisal and analysis stages and conclusions about the safety and performance of the device in question. The data shall support the assessment of the conformity of the device.

The clinical background should be presented, together with a description of current knowledge and State of the Art. The clinical evidence together with non-clinical data generated from non-clinical testing methods and other relevant documentation shall allow the manufacturer to demonstrate conformity with the general safety and performance requirements and shall be part of the technical documentation for the device in question. The clinical evaluation report should contain sufficient information to be read as a “standalone document” by an independent party (NCA, NB or other Regulatory Authority).

The report should include an outline of:

-   the technology on which the medical device is based, the intended use of the device and any claims made about the device’s clinical performance or safety
-   the nature and extent of the clinical data that has been evaluated
-   how the referenced information demonstrates the clinical performance and safety of the product.

The report should outline the conclusions reached about the safety and performance of the device from the evaluation, with respect to the intended use of the device. For each proposed clinical indication, it shall be stated whether:

-   conformity with the General Safety and Performance Requirements has been established
-   the performance and safety of the device as claimed have been established
-   the level of acceptability of side-effects has been established; and
-   the risks associated with the use of the device are acceptable when weighed against the benefits to the patient

Qualification of the responsible evaluators should be stated, and declarations of interests of the evaluators should contain statements that clarify the extent of the declaration.

The CER is a living document detailing the clinical evaluation of a device throughout its life-cycle. The planned frequency for updating the clinical evaluation should be stated in the plan or report. For class III devices, this is at least annually, see section 7.

For further guidance, see MEDDEV 2.7/1, rev.4 (Ref 2).

1.  N/A Summary of Safety and Clinical Performance

In addition to CERs, a public Summary of Safety and Clinical Performance (SSCP; MDR, Article 32) is required for class III and implantable devices, other than custom-made or investigational devices. This summary is expected to consider diagnostic or therapeutic options addressed in the CER and diagnostic/therapeutic alternatives. The SSCP shall be written in a way that is clear to the intended user and, if relevant, to the patient and shall be made available to the public via Eudamed.

The draft of the SSCP shall be part of the documentation to be submitted to the notified body involved in the conformity assessment and shall be validated by that body which then uploads the summary to Eudamed. The manufacturer shall mention on the label or instructions for use where the summary is available.

The SSCP shall include at least the following aspects:

1.  the identification of the device and the manufacturer, including unique device identification
2.  the intended purpose of the device and any indications, contraindications and target populations;
3.  a description of the device, including a reference to previous generation(s) or variants if such exist, and a description of the differences, as well as, where relevant, a description of any accessories, other devices and products, which are intended to be used in combination with the device;
4.  possible diagnostic or therapeutic alternatives;
5.  reference to any harmonised standards and Common Specifications applied;
6.  the summary of clinical evaluation and relevant information on post-market clinical follow-up;
7.  suggested profile and training for users;
8.  information on any residual risks and any undesirable effects, warnings and precautions.

    Any information written directly to patients or layman users shall be validated.

9.  Frequency of updates

The frequency at which the clinical evaluation needs to be updated should be defined and justified in the clinical evaluation plan (or report).

The frequency should consider:

-   whether the device carries significant risks (e.g. based on design, materials, components, invasiveness, clinical procedures, high-risk anatomical locations, high-risk target populations (e.g. pediatrics, elderly), severity of disease/ treatment challenges)
-   whether the device is well established, taking into consideration:
-   innovation;
-   relevant changes in clinical sciences, materials sciences or other sciences related to the device under evaluation;
-   the current level of confidence in the evaluation of clinical performance and clinical safety of the device; the manufacturer should consider
    -   the data available from clinical investigations, PMCF studies, registries or other systematic studies (including the number of devices used, if that usage was representative of the usage in the market, the results to date);
        -   the total number of devices used so far in the market, and expected reporting rates under the vigilance system
-   whether there are risks and uncertainties or unanswered questions, in the medium or long term, that would influence the frequency of updates
-   design changes or changes to manufacturing procedures (if any).

The clinical evaluation shall be updated:

-   when new information is received from PMS and/or PMCF that have an impact on the current evaluation or at least;
    -   every year second for class IIa devices, in conjunction with the update of the PSUR.
    -   every 2 to 5 years for class I devices, in conjunction with update of the PMS-report.
    -   N/A at least annually for class IIb and class III devices, in conjunction with the update of the PSUR.

Note that for class III devices devices, the PMCF evaluation report and, if indicated, the summary of safety and clinical performance shall be updated at least annually with such data.

Data generated through PMS and/or PMCF and other relevant data shall be analyzed and evaluated to serve as input for review of the clinical evaluation. New available data from literature and/or clinical investigations shall also be taken into consideration when reviewing the report.

1.  Post-Market Surveillance and Post-Market Clinical Follow-Up
2.  Post-Market Clinical Follow-Up

The clinical evaluation and its documentation shall be updated throughout the life cycle of the device concerned with clinical data obtained from the implementation of the manufacturer's PMCF plan in accordance with Part B of Annex XIV and the post-market surveillance plan referred to in Article 84 of the MDR.

PMCF shall be a continuous process that updates the clinical evaluation. When conducting PMCF, clinical data shall be proactively collected and evaluated from the use in or on humans of a device which bears the CE marking and is placed on the market or put into service within its intended purpose as referred to in the relevant conformity assessment procedure, with the aim of confirming safety and performance throughout the expected lifetime of the device, of ensuring the continued acceptability of identified risks and of detecting emerging risks on the basis of factual evidence.

PMCF shall be performed pursuant to a documented method laid down in a PMCF plan which shall be a part of the manufacturers overall PMS-plan referred to in Annex III in MDR (Ref 1).

The PMCF plan shall specify the methods and procedures for proactively collecting and evaluating clinical data with the aim of:

1.  confirming the safety and performance of the device throughout its expected lifetime,
2.  identifying previously unknown side-effects and monitoring the identified side-effects and contraindications,
3.  identifying and analysing emergent risks on the basis of factual evidence,
4.  ensuring the continued acceptability of the benefit-risk ratio, and
5.  identifying possible systematic misuse or off-label use of the device, with a view to verifying that the intended purpose is correct.

The PMCF plan shall include at least:

1.  the general methods and procedures of the PMCF to be applied, such as gathering of clinical experience gained, feedback from users, screening of scientific literature and of other sources of clinical data;[^7]
2.  the specific methods and procedures of PMCF to be applied, such as evaluation of suitable registers or PMCF studies;
3.  a rationale for the appropriateness of the methods and procedures referred to in points (a) and (b);
4.  a reference to the relevant parts of the clinical evaluation report and to risk management;
5.  the specific objectives to be addressed by the PMCF;
6.  an evaluation of the clinical data relating to equivalent or similar devices;
7.  reference to any relevant common specifications and harmonized standards when used, and relevant guidance on PMCF; and
8.  a detailed and adequately justified time schedule for PMCF activities (e.g. analysis of PMCF data and reporting to be undertaken by the manufacturer.

[^7]: If any of the activities is already described in the clinical evaluation or PMS plan and performed as part of the clinical evaluation or PMS, these activities could only be referenced in the PMCF plan.

The PMCF plan could be a part of the PMS plan.

The findings of PMCF shall be presented in the clinical evaluation report and shall also serve as input to risk management and corrective and preventive actions as appropriate.

1.  Post-Market Surveillance

The post market surveillance system referred to in Article 83 of MDR shall be based on a post market surveillance plan which shall be part of the technical documentation.

Manufacturers of class I devices shall prepare a PMS report summarising the results and conclusions of the analyses of the PMS data gathered as a result of the PMS plan together with a rationale and description of any preventive and corrective actions taken. The report shall be updated when necessary and made available to the competent authority upon request. The PMS report shall be included in the technical documentation and upon request be made available to competent authorities.

1.  Post-Market Surveillance and Periodic Safety Update Report

Manufacturers of class IIa devices, class IIb and class III shall prepare a periodic safety update report (PSUR) for each device and where relevant for each category or group of devices summarising the results and conclusions of the analyses of the PMS data gathered as a result of the PMS plan together with a rationale and description of any preventive and corrective actions taken. Throughout the lifetime of the device concerned, the PSUR shall set out;

(a) the conclusions of the benefit-risk determination;

(b) the main findings of the PMCF; and

(c) the volume of sales of the device and an estimate evaluation of the size and other characteristics of the population using the device and, where practicable, the usage frequency of the device.

Manufacturers of class IIa devices shall update the PSUR when necessary but at least every two years. The PSUR shall be included in the technical documentation and be made available to the notified body involved in the conformity assessment and, upon request, to competent authorities.

Manufacturers of class IIb and class III devices shall update the PSUR at least annually. That PSUR shall, except in the case of custom-made devices, be part of the technical documentation as specified in Annexes II and III.

2.  **N/A** Submission of the Periodic Safety Update Report

For class III devices or implantable devices, manufacturers shall submit PSURs by means of the electronic system referred to in Article 92 to the notified body involved in the conformity assessment in accordance with Article 52. 1.  N/A Clinical Evaluation Consultation Procedure for Certain Class III Devices

According to Article 61 paragraph 2, before the clinical evaluation is finalized, or before any clinical investigation is started, the manufacturer may consult an expert panel as referred to in Article 106. The consultations step is applicable for all class III devices and class IIb devices referred to in point (b) of Article 54(1). The purpose of the consultation is that the expert panel can review the manufacturer's intended clinical development strategy and proposals for clinical investigation. If consultation is performed, the manufacturer shall give due consideration to the views expressed by the expert panel and the consideration shall be documented in the clinical evaluation report.
