# Technical Report - Implementation of ISO 13485 for SaMD

## 1. Purpose

This technical report describes an interpretation model for implementing ISO 13485 in an organization developing Software as a Medical Device (SaMD).

The purpose is to clarify how ISO 13485 quality management system requirements can be implemented in a software-native environment without creating unnecessary duplication between written QMS procedures and controlled product-development evidence.

The report focuses particularly on:

- dynamic software processes;
- product/software change management;
- QMS change management;
- technical documentation as part of the product baseline;
- evidence generated by controlled development systems;
- risk-based application of formal change records.

## 2. Background

ISO 13485 is built around the idea that product quality is achieved through controlled processes. In traditional medical-device manufacturing, it is often possible to separate the written procedure, the physical product, and the production record.

In SaMD, this separation is less clear. The work of creating the product, controlling the process, and generating the evidence often happens in the same controlled software-development systems.

For example, the following may simultaneously be product work, process execution, and QMS evidence:

- work items;
- source code and configuration;
- pull or merge requests;
- code review;
- automated tests;
- CI/CD gates;
- release records;
- risk records;
- verification and validation evidence;
- post-market data;
- CAPA records;
- technical documentation.

The QMS must therefore explain how software-development records are used as controlled process evidence, rather than assuming that process control is shown only through standalone procedures and separate records.

## 3. Core Position

The proposed SaMD implementation model is:

- The QMS defines the control model.
- The controlled development system provides the primary execution evidence.
- Product/software changes are managed through product lifecycle controls.
- Technical documentation is part of the medical-device product baseline and is managed as product evidence.
- Separate QMS change-management records are required when the global process model changes.

This approach preserves the ISO 13485 requirement to control QMS processes while avoiding unnecessary formal change-management burden for every operational adjustment in an agile software environment.

## 4. Quality Assurance Disciplines for SaMD

Process control is one control type within quality assurance. It is important, but it is not a complete quality assurance discipline by itself.

For SaMD, quality assurance is the combined effect of multiple disciplines. These disciplines should be based on recognized QMS, software lifecycle, risk management, and software product quality concepts. The QMS should identify the relevant disciplines, define how they interact, and justify their relative weight based on risk, evidence strength, and the failure modes being controlled.

### 4.1 Quality Assurance Discipline Model

Quality assurance disciplines define what the organization must assure.

| Quality assurance discipline | What it assures | Typical evidence | Relative weight in SaMD QA |
|---|---|---|---|
| Governance and quality accountability | Quality responsibilities, authority, resources, independence, management oversight, and accountability are defined and effective | Quality policy, roles and responsibilities, management review, objectives, quality planning, escalation records | High. Governance determines whether the QMS can make and enforce quality decisions |
| Risk management and risk acceptance | Product, process, lifecycle, clinical, security, usability, and compliance risks are identified, controlled, verified, monitored, and accepted | Risk analysis, risk-control verification, benefit-risk rationale, residual-risk acceptance, PMS risk review | Very high. Risk determines the required strength and type of controls |
| Software lifecycle quality management | Requirements, design, implementation, integration, verification, validation, release, maintenance, and decommissioning are controlled as one lifecycle | Lifecycle plans, requirements, design records, implementation records, V&V evidence, release records, maintenance records | Very high. The lifecycle is the primary realization path for SaMD |
| Product quality engineering | The software product has the required functional suitability, reliability, usability, performance, compatibility, security, maintainability, adaptability, and safety | Architecture decisions, design reviews, test evidence, static analysis, security review, usability evidence, defect trends | Very high. Software quality is largely achieved through product engineering controls |
| Verification and validation | Product and process outputs meet requirements, intended use, risk controls, and acceptance criteria | Verification records, validation records, test results, clinical/performance validation, usability validation, release acceptance | Very high. V&V is primary evidence that outputs are acceptable |
| Configuration and change management | Product, documentation, tools, infrastructure, and evidence baselines are identified, controlled, changed, released, and recoverable | Baselines, version history, change records, release records, approval records, rollback records | High. Software quality depends on knowing what changed, why it changed, and which baseline is controlled |
| Evidence and traceability management | Claims, requirements, risks, controls, implementation, verification, validation, release, and post-market evidence can be connected and audited | Traceability records, requirement links, risk-control links, technical documentation, audit trails | High. Traceability makes the control logic visible and auditable |
| Competence and decision quality | Quality-critical decisions are made by competent roles with appropriate clinical, regulatory, technical, security, usability, and risk input | Competence criteria, role assignments, training records, review records, decision records, expert approvals | High where quality depends on expert judgment |
| Supplier, tool, and infrastructure assurance | External services, tools, libraries, infrastructure, automated systems, and suppliers are controlled according to their effect on product quality and safety | Supplier qualification, monitoring, contracts, SOUP/library assessment, tool validation, infrastructure records | Medium to high depending on dependency criticality |
| Monitoring, feedback, and improvement | Real-world use, internal operation, audits, complaints, incidents, telemetry, PMS, and CAPA feed learning and correction | PMS, complaints, incidents, vigilance, telemetry, trend analysis, CAPA, metrics, audit records, management review | High. Feedback closes the quality loop and detects drift |
| Adaptation and continuous improvement | The QMS, product, processes, documentation, tooling, and operating model can change safely in response to new evidence, product needs, regulation, and technical evolution | Retrospectives, improvement records, change assessments, CAPA links, PMS links, process updates, rationale for adaptation | High in software. Controlled adaptation is a quality mechanism, not a deviation from control |
| Simplicity and control effectiveness | Controls are justified by risk and effectiveness, and unnecessary procedural burden is avoided | Control justifications, process tailoring rationale, management review, audit observations, improvement records | Medium to high. Excessive control burden can obscure important controls and weaken adherence |

The relative weight is not universal. It shall be adjusted based on the SaMD product, intended use, risk class, clinical context, software architecture, operating model, dependency model, and post-market evidence.

### 4.2 Control Type Model

Control types define how assurance is achieved. A quality assurance discipline may be supported by one or more control types.

| Control type | Primary purpose | Typical evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Procedural control | Ensure consistency of human activity where the way of working is the main risk control | Process descriptions, procedures, work instructions, responsibilities, training records, completed records |
| Competence control | Ensure that qualified judgment is applied where quality depends on expertise rather than mechanical procedure | Competence criteria, role assignments, training records, experience records, expert review evidence |
| Review and approval control | Ensure independent, role-based, or cross-functional confirmation before acceptance, release, escalation, or closure | Review records, approval records, sign-offs, decision logs, meeting minutes, audit trails |
| Product/software lifecycle control | Control the process output through the controlled software-development lifecycle rather than through a separate procedural record | Work items, traceability, code/configuration records, pull/merge requests, test results, release records |
| Automated control | Ensure that a tool or system prevents, detects, enforces, records, or blocks required behavior | CI/CD results, automated checks, static analysis, access controls, templates, workflow rules, tool validation |
| Output V&V control | Demonstrate that outputs are correct, complete, suitable for intended use, and safe for release or use | Verification evidence, validation evidence, test results, acceptance records, usability or clinical/performance validation |
| Monitoring and feedback control | Detect product, process, safety, performance, usability, and compliance signals from real-world use and internal operation | PMS, complaints, incidents, vigilance, telemetry, trend analysis, process metrics, audit observations, management review |
| CAPA and escalation control | Ensure detected failures are corrected, prevented from recurring, escalated when required, and checked for effectiveness | CAPA records, root cause analysis, escalation records, correction records, preventive actions, effectiveness checks |
| Technical-documentation control | Control outputs that form part of the device product baseline or regulatory substantiation | Technical documentation, clinical evaluation, risk management file, V&V summary, GSPR evidence, document approval records |
| Adaptation control | Ensure the organization can safely adapt product, process, documentation, tooling, and operating model based on new needs or evidence | Retrospectives, improvement records, change assessments, CAPA links, PMS links, process updates, rationale for adaptation |

The QMS shall not assume that procedural control is the default or strongest form of control. For each process, the organization should identify the control type, or combination of control types, that most effectively controls the relevant risk.

### 4.3 Weighting Principle

The QMS should explicitly state which quality assurance disciplines provide the primary assurance for each process or product lifecycle area, and which control types are used to achieve that assurance.

For example:

- release quality may be weighted toward software lifecycle quality management, verification and validation, configuration and change management, risk management and risk acceptance, and monitoring;
- clinical or prepopulated-content quality may be weighted toward competence and decision quality, verification and validation, risk management and risk acceptance, monitoring, feedback, and CAPA;
- technical documentation quality may be weighted toward evidence and traceability management, configuration and change management, competence and decision quality, and regulatory review;
- complaint handling may be weighted toward monitoring, feedback, improvement, risk management, governance, and CAPA;
- configuration quality may be weighted toward configuration and change management, product quality engineering, verification and validation, monitoring, and automated controls;
- process improvement may be weighted toward adaptation and continuous improvement, simplicity and control effectiveness, monitoring, CAPA, and management review.

This avoids treating process control as the default answer for every quality question. Instead, QA disciplines and control types are combined according to risk and control effectiveness.

### 4.4 Weighting Record

For each core QMS or product lifecycle process, the organization should maintain a control-weighting rationale.

The rationale should identify:

- which QA disciplines are primary;
- which QA disciplines are supporting;
- which process or product failure modes are controlled;
- why the selected weighting is sufficient;
- what evidence demonstrates the controls;
- when the weighting must be reconsidered.

This control-weighting rationale may be documented in the process description, risk management file, technical documentation, management review, or a dedicated process-control matrix.

## 5. ISO 13485 Clause 4.1 as the Foundation

ISO 13485 clause 4.1 is the foundation for this implementation model.

In particular, clause 4.1 requires the organization to apply a risk-based approach to the control of the appropriate processes needed for the quality management system.

ISO 13485 is process oriented. It assumes that quality is achieved by defining, controlling, monitoring, and improving processes. This remains applicable to SaMD, but it is not sufficient by itself to explain how software quality is actually achieved.

For SaMD, product quality is not achieved first and foremost by procedural control alone. It is achieved through a combination of controls, including:

- product architecture;
- requirements and traceability;
- source-code control;
- configuration control;
- automated tests;
- code review;
- static analysis where applicable;
- verification and validation;
- release controls;
- runtime monitoring;
- post-market surveillance;
- CAPA feedback;
- risk-management updates;
- technical documentation as product evidence.

The implementation model must therefore diverge early from a purely process-procedure interpretation of ISO 13485. The divergence is not a rejection of process control. It is a risk-based allocation of control: some risks are best controlled by procedures, while other software quality risks are better controlled by product lifecycle controls, automation, V&V, technical documentation, and post-market feedback loops.

This is the central pillar:

Every process in the organization shall have an explicit justification for its level and type of control.

The justification shall explain:

- why the process is needed;
- what risk the process controls;
- what product, regulatory, clinical, security, operational, or QMS outcome the process protects;
- what records demonstrate that the process is performed;
- what level of review, approval, verification, validation, or monitoring is required;
- why the selected control level is sufficient;
- when escalation to stronger control is required.

The QMS shall not rely on implicit understanding that a process is low risk, administrative, dynamic, or product-controlled. The rationale for the control model shall be explicit.

From this point, process control diverges based on risk and on the most effective control type.

High-impact processes require stronger controls. Those controls may be procedural, technical, automated, verification-based, validation-based, monitoring-based, or release-based. Low-impact or administrative processes may use lighter controls, but the reason for the lighter control must still be documented.

### 5.1 Process Control Justification

Each process shall be assigned a documented control justification.

The control justification should consider:

- impact on patient safety;
- impact on device performance;
- impact on regulatory compliance;
- impact on clinical evaluation or claims;
- impact on risk management;
- impact on verification and validation;
- impact on post-market surveillance, vigilance, complaints, or CAPA;
- impact on release or deployment;
- impact on technical documentation;
- impact on suppliers, tools, infrastructure, or data integrity;
- detectability and reversibility of failure;
- frequency of change;
- complexity of the process;
- competence required to perform or review the process.

The output of the justification is the control level for the process.

Examples of control levels:

- policy-level control;
- procedure-level control;
- work-instruction control;
- product/software lifecycle control;
- technical-documentation control;
- automated tool/workflow control;
- review and approval control;
- validation or verification control;
- monitoring through metrics or PMS;
- CAPA or escalation control.

### 5.2 Control Type Selection

The control of a QMS process does not always need to be a procedure-first control.

For each process, the organization should determine which type of control is most effective for the risk being controlled. The control type may be:

- procedural control, where consistency of human activity is the main risk control;
- competence control, where qualified judgment is the main risk control;
- review/approval control, where independent or role-based confirmation is sufficient;
- product/software lifecycle control, where the process output is controlled through work items, traceability, code/configuration, tests, and release records;
- automated control, where a tool or system enforces the required behavior;
- output V&V control, where verification and validation detect or prevent defective, incomplete, unsafe, or unsuitable outputs;
- monitoring and feedback control, where trends, PMS, complaints, telemetry, metrics, audits, or management review detect drift or emerging weakness;
- CAPA and escalation control, where failures are accepted as detectable but require systematic correction, prevention, escalation, and effectiveness checks;
- technical-documentation control, where the controlled output is part of the device product baseline or regulatory substantiation;
- adaptation control, where the process is allowed to evolve within defined boundaries and escalation criteria;
- technical excellence and design quality control, where maintainable architecture, good design, security, testability, observability, and simplicity prevent defects and enable safe change;
- simplicity and control-burden management, where unnecessary controls are avoided unless they reduce risk, improve quality, strengthen evidence, or support compliance;
- supplier, tool, and infrastructure control, where external services, tools, libraries, infrastructure, and automated systems are controlled according to dependency criticality.

The selected control type should match the failure mode.

Examples:

- A complaint-handling process may require procedural control, role assignment, timelines, and records.
- A release process may rely heavily on automated tests, CI/CD gates, approval records, and release evidence.
- A configuration process may rely on templates, automated validation, peer review, and production monitoring.
- A technical-documentation process may rely on traceability to product version, regulatory review, and product-baseline approval.
- A prepopulated-content process may rely on clinical competence, review, validation, PMS, and CAPA feedback.

The QMS should explicitly state when software lifecycle controls are accepted as the primary process control.

### 5.3 Process Control Criteria

The QMS should define control criteria that allow each process to be assigned a control level.

The control level should be based on risk, not tradition. A process should receive stronger control when failure could affect patient safety, device performance, regulatory compliance, data integrity, release integrity, technical documentation, or the ability to detect and correct product problems.

| Control level | Process risk profile | Typical control criteria | Typical controls | Typical evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Level 0 - Minimal control | Administrative process with no credible impact on product quality, safety, performance, regulatory compliance, or QMS effectiveness | Failure is easily detectable, reversible, and does not affect controlled product or QMS records | Basic ownership, simple instruction or convention, document approval if documented | Version history, owner approval, basic record trail |
| Level 1 - Documented routine control | Routine QMS or support process with indirect or low impact | Failure may create inefficiency or local inconsistency but is unlikely to affect product baseline, patient safety, regulatory compliance, or release decisions | Procedure or work instruction, role assignment, review/approval, basic records | Approved procedure, completed records, review history |
| Level 2 - Controlled lifecycle process | Process output contributes to product/software lifecycle, technical documentation, release, risk control, PMS, CAPA, or complaint handling | Failure may affect traceability, product evidence, release quality, or ability to demonstrate compliance | Defined workflow, competent review, traceability, product/software lifecycle records, risk-based checks | Work items, review records, traceability links, release records, technical documentation records |
| Level 3 - Risk-critical process | Process failure may affect device safety, performance, clinical claims, risk acceptability, regulatory submissions, vigilance, CAPA effectiveness, or released software behavior | Failure may not be fully detectable by routine review, or may require independent assessment before acceptance | Formal procedure, impact assessment, independent or cross-functional review, V&V, risk review, approval gate, monitoring | Impact assessment, V&V evidence, risk record, approval record, release decision, PMS/CAPA linkage |
| Level 4 - High-risk or regulated decision process | Process failure may directly create unacceptable patient risk, regulatory noncompliance, invalid technical documentation, unsafe release, or failure to meet legal reporting obligations | Failure requires formal justification, documented acceptance, and escalation if controls fail | Formal process, defined acceptance criteria, independent approval, validation where applicable, management or PRRC/regulatory involvement, CAPA escalation | Formal change record, signed approval, validation record, regulatory assessment, management/PRRC review, CAPA or escalation record |

The control level should be documented for each process. The record should explain why the selected level is sufficient and what would trigger a higher level of control.

The control criteria should also allow mixed control. A process may have low procedural control but high automated, V&V, or monitoring control. For example, a configuration process may not require a long written procedure for every change if configuration templates, peer review, automated validation, regression testing, release gates, and monitoring adequately control the relevant failure modes.

### 5.4 Risk Acceptance for Process Control

Risk-based process control requires a risk acceptance system.

The organization shall define how process-related risks are evaluated and when the selected control level is acceptable. The question is not only whether a process can fail, but whether the residual risk of that process failure is acceptable after considering the controls that detect, prevent, correct, or mitigate the failure.

For each process, the control justification should identify:

- relevant process failure modes;
- potential impact of those failure modes;
- existing controls that prevent the failure;
- existing controls that detect the failure;
- existing controls that mitigate the effect of the failure;
- residual risk after controls;
- acceptance rationale for the residual risk;
- escalation criteria if the residual risk is no longer acceptable.

The risk acceptance system for process control should define criteria such as:

- unacceptable process risk requiring formal procedural control, independent review, validation, escalation, or CAPA;
- conditionally acceptable process risk requiring monitoring, review, or periodic evaluation;
- acceptable process risk controlled through routine lifecycle evidence;
- negligible process risk controlled through document approval or basic record traceability.

Risk acceptance shall be justified by evidence, not by assertion. A statement that a process is low risk, administrative, or adequately controlled shall be supported by the process-control justification.

### 5.5 Software V&V as a Control for Process Failure Modes

In SaMD, software verification and validation may control failure modes that originate in other processes.

For example, a weakness in configuration, requirement specification, prepopulated-content maintenance, release preparation, or user-interface wording may be controlled by:

- automated tests;
- system tests;
- regression tests;
- test data review;
- clinical content verification;
- usability validation;
- release validation;
- monitoring of production behavior;
- PMS or CAPA feedback loops.

This means that some process risks may be accepted with lighter procedural control if strong software V&V controls detect or prevent the relevant failure modes before the device is released or before harm can occur.

However, this control strategy must be explicit. The organization shall document:

- which process failure mode is controlled by software V&V;
- which V&V activity detects, prevents, or mitigates the failure mode;
- why the V&V control is sufficient;
- where the V&V evidence is recorded;
- what happens if the V&V control fails;
- whether the V&V control itself requires validation or monitoring.

Software V&V does not eliminate the need for process control. It may justify a different control path when it demonstrably controls the relevant process failure mode.

### 5.6 Evaluation and Justification Records

Evaluations and justifications are themselves important QMS evidence.

When the organization chooses a lighter control path, the record shall explain why the lighter path is appropriate. When the organization chooses a stronger control path, the record shall explain what risk or regulatory need requires it.

This applies especially to:

- classification of process changes;
- decision not to open a formal QMS change record;
- decision to control a change through product/software lifecycle records;
- decision to treat technical documentation as product-baseline evidence;
- decision that a change has no product, regulatory, clinical, or QMS impact;
- decision that no immediate risk-management file update is needed after post-market input;
- decision that document review/approval is sufficient evidence.

The goal is not to create excessive paperwork. The goal is to make the risk-based control logic visible and auditable.

## 6. SaMD Process Characteristics

### 6.1 Dynamic Processes

Some SaMD processes are intentionally dynamic. They continuously adapt based on:

- product needs;
- product risk;
- customer use;
- development learning;
- PMS and PMCF data;
- CAPA outcomes;
- audit outcomes;
- management review;
- regulatory changes;
- tooling and automation maturity.

For such processes, change is not always an exceptional event. Continuous adjustment is part of the intended process design.

Dynamic does not mean uncontrolled. A dynamic process requires a stronger explanation of its control model, because the evidence of control may be distributed across tools, tickets, code, tests, release records, PMS, CAPA, and risk-management records.

For each dynamic process, the QMS shall define:

- what parts of the process are allowed to change continuously;
- what records show those changes;
- what risk controls constrain those changes;
- what triggers formal review or escalation;
- when accumulated operational changes require an update to the process description;
- who is responsible for monitoring whether the process remains effective.

### 6.2 Candidate Dynamic Processes

The following processes may be dynamic by design:

- software development and implementation;
- release and deployment;
- configuration management;
- technical documentation maintenance;
- risk-management updates driven by product evidence;
- PMS/CAPA-driven product improvement;
- tooling, templates, and workflow automation used to execute controlled work.

These processes must still be controlled. The control is often demonstrated through execution records rather than separate narrative justification for every operational adjustment.

For each candidate dynamic process, the organization shall document why dynamic operation is appropriate and what control path applies.

## 7. Control Model

### 7.1 QMS Documents

QMS procedures define:

- responsibilities;
- required activities;
- decision criteria;
- required records;
- interfaces between processes;
- review and approval gates;
- regulatory obligations;
- escalation points.

QMS documents should describe the control model at the level necessary to ensure repeatability, accountability, and auditability.

They should not duplicate every operational detail already controlled more effectively in development tooling.

For example, a QMS procedure for record retention or document control does not need to restate every retention mechanism used in GitLab, GitHub, Jira, or equivalent controlled systems. The procedure should define the control intent, responsibilities, record types, retention expectations, access expectations, and escalation criteria. Evidence that the required records are actually retained, versioned, reviewable, access controlled, and retrievable may be provided by the controlled system itself.

### 7.2 Controlled Development System

The controlled development system may provide primary evidence for process execution, including:

- work items or issue-tracking records;
- Git commits and pull requests;
- code review records;
- configuration review records;
- automated test results;
- CI/CD pipeline results;
- release records;
- deployment records;
- verification and validation evidence;
- traceability links;
- risk-control implementation evidence;
- CAPA and PMS records.

Where these records are controlled, retained, reviewable, and traceable, they can serve as QMS records.

This is especially relevant for record retention and document control. If GitLab, GitHub, Jira, or another controlled system provides durable records, version history, audit trail, access control, review status, timestamps, and retrievability, the retained content in that system can be sufficient evidence of control. The QMS procedure should not be required to reproduce the operational implementation details when the system evidence demonstrates that the control is effective.

## 8. Technical Documentation as Product Baseline

Technical documentation is not ordinary QMS paperwork.

Technical documentation is part of the medical-device product baseline. It substantiates the device and must be controlled as product evidence.

Changes to technical documentation are assessed in relation to:

- affected product version;
- intended purpose and claims;
- risk management file;
- clinical evaluation;
- PMS/PMCF and PSUR evidence;
- verification and validation evidence;
- release state;
- regulatory submissions and change notices;
- declarations of conformity;
- information for safety and user-facing documentation.

QMS procedures describe the system used to create, maintain, release, monitor, and support the device. Technical documentation describes and substantiates the device itself.

Therefore, technical-documentation changes should be managed as product/software changes where they affect the medical-device baseline.

## 9. Change Categories

The QMS should distinguish between different types of change.

### 9.1 Administrative or Editorial Update

An administrative or editorial update is a change with no impact on required activities, responsibilities, decision criteria, records, regulatory interfaces, product controls, or QMS effectiveness.

Examples:

- typo correction;
- formatting change;
- broken link update;
- document structure improvement;
- wording clarification that does not change meaning;
- metadata correction.

Evidence may be limited to document version history and competent review/approval.

### 9.2 Continuous Operational Adjustment

A continuous operational adjustment is an expected adaptation inside a defined dynamic process.

Examples:

- refinement of a ticket template;
- adjustment of a CI check;
- improvement to a test script;
- change in workflow automation;
- minor clarification in a release checklist;
- improved traceability link structure.

These adjustments are controlled through the relevant product/software lifecycle records unless they alter the global process model.

### 9.3 Product or Software Change

A product or software change affects the medical device, software, configuration, prepopulated content, risk controls, verification/validation evidence, release state, or product baseline.

Examples:

- software feature change;
- configuration change;
- prepopulated-content update;
- risk-control implementation;
- change to release behavior;
- change to user-facing information for safety;
- technical-documentation update linked to the product baseline.

These changes are managed through product lifecycle controls.

### 9.4 Technical-Documentation Change

A technical-documentation change affects documentation that substantiates the medical device.

Examples:

- risk management report update;
- clinical evaluation report update;
- PMS/PMCF plan or report update;
- software architecture update;
- verification or validation report update;
- GSPR evidence update;
- declaration-related evidence update.

These changes are managed as product-baseline changes and assessed for product, regulatory, clinical, and risk impact.

### 9.5 QMS Process Change

A QMS process change alters when, why, or how controlled work is performed.

Examples:

- changed process ownership;
- changed required activities;
- changed approval gates;
- changed required records;
- changed regulatory interfaces;
- changed CAPA/vigilance/PMS workflow;
- changed release criteria;
- changed tool validation approach;
- changed supplier control approach.

Formal QMS change management is triggered when the global process model changes or may affect QMS effectiveness, regulatory compliance, or the devices produced under the QMS.

## 10. Risk-Based Change Management

Formal QMS change management should be triggered by potential impact, not by the mere fact that a document version changes.

During review of a document, process, tool, or workflow update, the reviewer should classify the change as one of:

- administrative/editorial update;
- continuous operational adjustment;
- product/software change;
- technical-documentation change;
- QMS process change.

For each non-trivial update, the record should show enough rationale to explain why the selected control path is appropriate.

The rationale should include risk acceptance where relevant. If a change remains under routine lifecycle control rather than formal QMS change control, the record should explain why the residual process risk is acceptable and what controls provide that acceptance.

## 11. Formal QMS Change Triggers

A formal QMS change-management record should be opened when a change affects or may affect:

- responsibilities or role ownership;
- required process activities;
- required records;
- review or approval gates;
- release criteria;
- risk-management controls;
- regulatory compliance interfaces;
- PMS, vigilance, CAPA, complaint, or audit obligations;
- supplier or outsourcing controls;
- tool validation approach;
- interaction between product lifecycle processes;
- effectiveness of the QMS.

## 12. Software V&V as Cross-Process Control

Software V&V may serve as a cross-process control when a process failure mode can be detected, prevented, or mitigated through verification, validation, or release testing.

This is especially relevant for SaMD because many organizational processes produce outputs that become software behavior, configuration, prepopulated content, product documentation, or release artifacts.

Examples:

- A requirements-process weakness may be controlled by traceability review, test coverage, and validation against intended use.
- A configuration-process weakness may be controlled by automated configuration tests and release validation.
- A prepopulated-content process weakness may be controlled by clinical review, test cases, and post-market monitoring.
- A release-process weakness may be controlled by CI/CD gates, regression testing, release checklists, and deployment verification.
- A documentation-process weakness may be controlled by traceability to product version, review gates, and consistency checks.

When software V&V is used as a process control, the organization should document:

- the process failure mode;
- the V&V control;
- the evidence generated by the control;
- the acceptance criterion;
- the residual risk;
- the escalation path if the V&V control fails.

This creates a defensible link between ISO 13485 process control and software lifecycle evidence.

## 13. Evidence Model

Depending on the change category, acceptable evidence may include:

- document version history;
- review and approval records;
- reviewer rationale;
- issue-tracking record or QMS change record;
- retained records in a controlled system such as GitLab, GitHub, Jira, or equivalent tooling;
- product/software change ticket;
- pull request or merge request;
- release record;
- verification or validation evidence;
- process risk-acceptance rationale;
- evidence that software V&V controls relevant process failure modes;
- CAPA record;
- PMS or vigilance record;
- risk-management record;
- technical-documentation change record;
- traceability links.

The evidence must be sufficient to show:

- what changed;
- why it changed;
- who reviewed and approved it;
- whether product safety/performance or regulatory compliance was affected;
- whether residual process risk remains acceptable;
- whether further verification, validation, risk review, regulatory action, or training was needed.

## 14. Agile and ISO 13485 Compatibility

Agile software development is compatible with ISO 13485 when the organization defines how agile execution is controlled and evidenced.

The Agile Manifesto is relevant because it describes how software organizations create effective software in changing environments. Its values should not be read as rejecting documentation, process, contracts, or planning. The manifesto states a preference for the items on the left while still valuing the items on the right.

In a regulated SaMD context, the Agile Manifesto can be interpreted as follows:

- Individuals and interactions over processes and tools: competent collaboration is essential, but the QMS must still define roles, responsibilities, required records, and escalation paths.
- Working software over comprehensive documentation: working, verified, validated software is central evidence of quality, but technical documentation remains required as product evidence and regulatory substantiation.
- Customer collaboration over contract negotiation: clinical, customer, user, and post-market feedback are critical inputs, but contractual and regulatory obligations still define boundaries.
- Responding to change over following a plan: controlled adaptation is necessary for software quality, but changes must remain traceable, risk-assessed, verified, validated where needed, and reflected in the product baseline.

For SaMD, the Agile Manifesto supports the argument that quality is not created by static process descriptions alone. Quality is created through short feedback loops, working software, review, verification, validation, user feedback, and post-market learning. ISO 13485 requires these ways of working to be made controlled, repeatable, risk based, and auditable.

The conflict is not between agility and ISO 13485 as such. The conflict arises when:

- the QMS describes static procedural control;
- the organization operates through dynamic software tooling;
- the QMS does not explain which development records are controlled QMS evidence;
- auditors cannot see how process changes are evaluated.

The QMS must therefore describe how agile work is controlled:

- work items define planned work;
- code/configuration changes implement controlled changes;
- reviews and approvals provide competence checks;
- automated tests and CI/CD provide repeatable verification and may control defined process failure modes;
- release records establish deployment history;
- risk and CAPA records link post-market evidence back into product controls;
- technical documentation is maintained as part of the product baseline.

### 14.1 Agile Values as QMS Design Constraints

The QMS should be designed so that regulatory control does not destroy the mechanisms by which software quality is achieved.

This means:

- keep procedures as control models, not exhaustive descriptions of every operational detail;
- let controlled tools generate primary execution evidence where appropriate;
- make verification and validation central to process risk acceptance;
- update process descriptions when the global way of working changes, not for every operational adjustment;
- preserve fast feedback loops while ensuring traceability and review;
- use post-market data and CAPA to improve both product and process;
- treat technical documentation as product evidence connected to the evolving product baseline.

### 14.2 Agile Practices as Controlled Records

Common agile practices can become controlled QMS records when they are defined, retained, reviewable, and linked to relevant product or process evidence.

Examples:

- backlog items can define planned work and rationale;
- sprint or iteration outputs can show implementation history;
- pull/merge requests can show review, discussion, and approval;
- automated tests can show repeatable verification;
- release records can show deployment history and included changes;
- retrospectives can provide process-improvement input;
- defects, complaints, and incidents can feed CAPA and PMS;
- architecture decisions can justify technical and risk-control choices.

The QMS should define which agile records are formal QMS records and how they are retained.

## 15. Alignment of ISO 13485 Chapter 7 to SaMD Lifecycle Controls

ISO 13485 Chapter 7 addresses product realization. In a traditional physical-device context, this is often understood through distinct stages such as design, development, production, installation, servicing, and preservation.

For SaMD, those concepts remain applicable in intent, but the operational separation is different. Modern software is continuously specified, implemented, verified, released, deployed, monitored, and improved through controlled lifecycle systems.

The QMS should therefore map Chapter 7 requirements to software-centric lifecycle controls rather than forcing a physical manufacturing model onto SaMD.

| ISO 13485 Chapter 7 concept | Traditional interpretation | SaMD interpretation | Typical SaMD controls and evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Product realization planning | Planning how the physical device will be produced and controlled | Planning the software lifecycle, release model, regulatory pathway, risk controls, V&V strategy, technical documentation, and post-market feedback loops | Product plans, regulatory conformity plans, development plans, release plans, risk management plan, V&V plan, PMS/PMCF plan |
| Customer-related processes | Determining and reviewing customer requirements, communication, complaints | Determining intended use, user needs, clinical workflow requirements, customer configuration needs, support pathways, and complaint intake | User needs, intended purpose, customer requirements, support records, complaint records, configuration requirements |
| Design and development planning | Planning design stages, reviews, verification, validation, transfer | Planning iterative software design, architecture, requirements, risk controls, implementation, review, test, validation, release readiness, and technical documentation updates | Backlog/work items, design plans, architecture records, requirements, traceability, review records, test plans, validation plans |
| Design and development inputs | Capturing product requirements | Capturing intended purpose, clinical requirements, software requirements, usability needs, security requirements, regulatory requirements, interoperability requirements, and risk-control requirements | Requirements repository, GSPR mapping, risk-control requirements, usability inputs, clinical inputs, cybersecurity inputs |
| Design and development outputs | Drawings, specifications, manufacturing instructions | Source code, configuration, prepopulated content, architecture, technical documentation, test specifications, release artifacts, user-facing information, deployment specifications | Code repository, configuration records, technical documentation, test cases, release package, IFU/user information |
| Design and development review | Formal design review at defined stages | Risk-based review of software increments, architecture, requirements, prepopulated content, risk controls, usability, V&V results, and release readiness | Pull/merge reviews, design reviews, clinical review, risk review, release review, technical documentation review |
| Design and development verification | Verifying outputs meet inputs | Demonstrating that software outputs meet requirements and risk-control specifications | Automated tests, system tests, regression tests, traceability, test reports, static analysis where applicable |
| Design and development validation | Confirming device meets intended use and user needs | Confirming the SaMD performs safely and effectively in intended clinical/user context | Clinical evaluation, usability validation, performance validation, simulated clinical cases, real-world evidence where applicable |
| Design and development transfer | Transfer from design to production | Controlled transition from approved software baseline to release/deployment pipeline and operational environment | Release approval, deployment checklist, release notes, build artifacts, deployment records, environment qualification |
| Control of design and development changes | Controlling design changes after initial approval | Controlling software, configuration, prepopulated content, risk, V&V, technical documentation, and release changes through lifecycle records | Change tickets, pull/merge requests, impact assessment, risk updates, test evidence, release records, technical documentation updates |
| Purchasing | Control of purchased product and suppliers | Control of suppliers, cloud services, development tools, libraries, outsourced services, data providers, and critical infrastructure | Supplier qualification, supplier monitoring, tool assessment, SOUP/library assessment, service agreements, security reviews |
| Production and service provision | Manufacturing, assembly, installation, servicing | Build, packaging, configuration, deployment, operation, monitoring, support, maintenance, patching, and controlled updates | CI/CD records, build logs, deployment records, monitoring records, service tickets, maintenance records, incident records |
| Cleanliness/contamination control | Physical contamination controls | Usually not directly applicable unless hardware or physical media is involved; may map to data integrity, environment separation, and contamination of datasets/configuration | Environment segregation, test/production separation, data-quality controls, configuration controls |
| Installation activities | Installing device at customer site | Deployment, activation, customer configuration, onboarding, integration, environment setup, and verification that deployed software matches approved baseline | Deployment records, onboarding checklist, configuration records, integration test evidence, environment records |
| Servicing activities | Repair, maintenance, field servicing | Software maintenance, support, patching, incident handling, security updates, customer support, and product updates | Support tickets, maintenance tickets, patch records, incident records, CAPA records, release records |
| Particular requirements for sterile devices | Sterility controls | Not applicable to pure SaMD unless combined with sterile physical devices | Applicability assessment |
| Validation of production and service processes | Validating processes where output cannot be fully verified later | Validating tools, pipelines, deployment processes, data migration, configuration processes, monitoring, and other automated or semi-automated controls where later inspection is insufficient | Tool validation, pipeline validation, deployment validation, configuration validation, backup/restore tests, migration validation |
| Identification | Identifying product status and version | Identifying software version, build, release, configuration, environment, technical documentation version, and deployed baseline | Version control, build IDs, release IDs, UDI where applicable, configuration records, deployment records |
| Traceability | Lot, batch, component traceability | Traceability across requirement, risk, code/configuration, test, release, technical documentation, complaint, incident, and CAPA | Traceability matrix, issue links, commit links, test links, release links, risk-control links |
| Customer property | Customer-owned physical property | Customer data, production data, configuration, integration credentials, customer-specific settings, and protected health information handled as processor/service provider | Data processing agreements, access control, audit logs, backup records, configuration records, security controls |
| Preservation of product | Preventing product damage/deterioration | Preserving source code, configuration, build artifacts, technical documentation, release records, risk records, logs, backups, and reproducible baselines | Repository retention, artifact storage, backup/restore, archival, access control, retention policy |
| Control of monitoring and measuring equipment | Calibration and control of measuring devices | Control of test tools, validation tools, monitoring tools, clinical/performance measurement methods, datasets, scripts, and automated test environments | Tool validation, test-environment control, dataset control, script review, monitoring-tool qualification |

The purpose of this mapping is not to remove Chapter 7 obligations. The purpose is to define their SaMD-equivalent controls and evidence.

Where a traditional Chapter 7 activity does not exist as a separate activity in SaMD, the QMS should document:

- why the traditional activity is not directly applicable;
- which SaMD lifecycle control satisfies the intent;
- what evidence demonstrates control;
- what risk acceptance rationale supports the control level;
- when escalation or additional control is required.

## 16. Proposed QMS Implementation

To implement this model, the QMS should:

1. Define explicit process-control justifications for all organizational processes.
2. Identify and weight the QA disciplines that provide primary and supporting controls for each process.
3. Assign each process to a documented process control level.
4. Define risk-acceptance criteria for process control.
5. Define how software V&V may control process failure modes.
6. Define dynamic processes explicitly.
7. Define change categories and examples.
8. Define when document review/approval is sufficient.
9. Define when product/software change management applies.
10. Define when technical-documentation change management applies.
11. Define when formal QMS change management applies.
12. Require documented rationale for non-trivial "no impact" conclusions.
13. Link product/software changes to risk, verification, validation, release, PMS, CAPA, and technical documentation where applicable.
14. Treat controlled development records as QMS records where they execute or evidence QMS processes.
15. Define which agile records are formal QMS records and how they are retained.
16. Maintain traceability sufficient for internal and external audit.

## 17. Draft Market Position

A SaMD organization should acknowledge that ISO 13485 requires QMS process changes to be evaluated for impact on the QMS, regulatory compliance, and the medical devices produced under the QMS.

The proposed practice is risk based and grounded in explicit risk acceptance. Formal QMS change management is required when the global process model changes or when a change may affect product safety/performance, regulatory compliance, QMS effectiveness, or the medical devices produced under the QMS.

In a software-native QMS, some processes are dynamic by design and continuously adapt to product needs. Operational adjustments within those processes are controlled through product/software lifecycle records, including work items, code/configuration, reviews, tests, risk controls, technical documentation, release records, PMS, and CAPA. Where justified, software V&V may act as a control for failure modes originating in other processes.

Technical documentation is considered part of the medical-device product baseline and is change managed as product evidence, not as ordinary QMS paperwork.

The QMS should define the categories of change, the risk-based trigger for formal QMS change records, and the evidence expected when a change is assessed as administrative, minor, or without impact.
